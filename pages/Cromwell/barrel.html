    Armor-Piercing (AP)

  • Description: A solid shot designed to penetrate the armor of enemy tanks and armored vehicles. Capability: The AP round was effective against lighter German tanks like the Panzer III and Panzer IV, and other lightly armored targets. It was less effective against heavier tanks, such as the German Panther or Tiger, especially at longer ranges.

    • High-Explosive (HE)

  • Description: A shell designed to explode upon impact, producing fragmentation and a powerful blast. Capability: The HE shell was used primarily against soft targets like infantry, unarmored vehicles, and fortifications. It was highly effective in suppressing infantry positions and causing damage to buildings and light vehicles.

    • High-Explosive Squash Head (HESH)

  • Description: A type of high-explosive round that used a soft, "squash" warhead designed to flatten against the target's armor before detonating. Capability: HESH rounds were particularly effective against British and Commonwealth tanks and armored vehicles.     • Smoke

  • Description: A shell filled with smoke-producing compounds, fired to create a smoke screen. Capability: Used to provide concealment for advancing or retreating units, or to obscure enemy vision and disrupt their targeting. Smoke rounds were an essential tool for battlefield tactics, especially during offensive operations or withdrawal.

    • Canister

  • Description: A shell packed with small metal balls, similar to a shotgun blast. Capability: The canister round was highly effective against infantry and light vehicles at close range. When fired, it spread a large number of projectiles over a wide area, effectively mowing down infantry or lightly armored targets in close combat.
