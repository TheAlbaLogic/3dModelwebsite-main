The T-34 tank's cannon, whether the 76.2mm F-34 or the later 85mm ZiS-S-53, was central to the tank's effectiveness on the battlefield. The F-34 offered good performance against early German tanks, but as German armour improved, the ZiS-S-53 85mm gun was introduced, giving the T-34 a significant boost in firepower. The cannon's relatively high rate of fire, combined with a variety of ammunition types, made the T-34 versatile in both offensive and defensive roles. The design of the gun and its mounting, along with the overall turret and recoil system, contributed to the T-34's reputation as one of the most effective and feared tanks of World War II.

Cannon Type: 85mm ZiS-S-53 Gun (Later Models)

Capability: As German tanks, such as the Tiger and Panther, started to become more heavily armoured, the T-34 was upgraded with the 85mm ZiS-S-53 gun. This newer gun offered significantly improved performance over the F-34, with better armour-piercing capabilities and a higher muzzle velocity. The 85mm gun provided a balance between firepower and weight, and was able to engage most German tanks effectively at longer ranges.

    Rate of Fire

  • Around 15-20 rounds per minute.
    Capability: The T-34's main cannon, whether the F-34 or ZiS-S-53, had a fairly high rate of fire, with experienced crews able to achieve a rate of around 15-20 rounds per minute. This made the T-34 highly effective in rapid engagements, allowing it to keep up a consistent level of suppressive fire, particularly in armoured battles. The higher rate of fire was advantageous when dealing with infantry or lightly armoured vehicles in addition to enemy tanks.

    • Armour-Piercing (AP) Shells

  • Ammunition: BR-350A (standard AP round) Capability: The BR-350A was the standard armour-piercing shell for the early T-34 with the 76.2mm F-34 gun. Designed to penetrate the armour of enemy tanks, it was highly effective against lighter German tanks such as the Panzer III and Panzer IV early in the war. This round had moderate penetration, but by 1943, its effectiveness started diminishing against heavier German tanks like the Tiger and Panther. The BR-350A was still useful against less-armoured targets, such as trucks, artillery, and infantry positions.

    • Armour-Piercing Capped (APC) Shells

  • Ammunition: BR-350B (APC round) Capability: The BR-350B was an armour-piercing capped shell, designed to improve penetration against thicker armour. The APC cap helped the round maintain its velocity and penetration power against sloped armour, a critical feature when engaging enemy tanks like the Panzer IV or early Panther tanks. These rounds were more effective than standard AP rounds against heavily armoured targets, providing a significant improvement in firepower for the T-34.

    • High-Explosive (HE) Shells

  • Ammunition: OF-350 (standard HE shell) Capability: The OF-350 high-explosive round was used for anti-infantry and anti-soft target roles. It was effective against infantry, artillery, and light vehicles or buildings, as the shell would explode on impact, dispersing shrapnel and causing significant damage to non-armoured targets. The HE shell was widely used in supporting infantry and clearing enemy fortifications during assaults. While the 76.2mm F-34 wasn't as effective against heavily armoured targets when using HE, it was useful for dealing with non-armoured or lightly armoured foes.

    • High-Explosive Anti-Tank (HEAT) Shells (85mm)

  • Ammunition: HT-365 Capability: The HT-365 was a HEAT round designed to penetrate thick armour, such as that found on the Tiger II and King Tiger tanks. Using a shaped charge, the HEAT round could effectively penetrate the thickest armour at close to medium ranges, giving the T-34 a strong countermeasure against the heaviest German tanks in the war. The HEAT round was especially important in the latter stages of the war when German tanks had increased their armour thickness significantly.
