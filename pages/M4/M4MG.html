The M4 Sherman’s machine guns played a critical role in increasing the tank’s combat effectiveness. The M1919A4 served primarily in anti-infantry and suppression roles, while the M2 .50 caliber machine gun was an excellent defense against air threats and light vehicles. The combination of these weapons, alongside the Sherman’s main armament, made it a versatile and effective tank on the battlefield.

    Coaxial Machine Gun (Browning M1919A4)

  • Type: .30 caliber M1919A4 Browning air-cooled machine gun. Capability: The M1919A4 was mounted coaxially with the Sherman’s main gun, meaning it was positioned next to the main barrel and aimed in the same direction. It was primarily used for anti-infantry roles, engaging enemy soldiers, light vehicles, and soft-skinned targets. It had a high rate of fire, making it effective for suppressive fire and creating a field of fire to protect the tank from enemy infantry assaults.

    • Hull-Mounted Machine Gun (Browning M1919A4)

  • Type: .30 caliber M1919A4 Browning air-cooled machine gun. Capability: A second M1919A4 was mounted in the hull of the Sherman tank, operated by the driver. It was typically used for engaging infantry, light vehicles, and providing additional fire support during assaults. The hull-mounted machine gun was controlled via a simple trigger mechanism, often aimed through a small vision slit or periscope.

    • Turret-Mounted Machine Gun (Browning M2 .50 caliber)

  • Type: .50 caliber M2 Browning heavy machine gun (mounted in the turret). Capability: The M2 .50 caliber machine gun was mounted on top of the Sherman’s turret and was used for engaging enemy infantry, aircraft, and lightly armoured vehicles. Its larger caliber and higher rate of fire made it highly effective in suppressing enemy positions and providing anti-aircraft defense, especially during low-level attacks. It could also be used against light vehicles and personnel at longer ranges

    • Ammunition Types for M1919A4 (.30 caliber)

  • Type: Ball Ammunition: Standard round used for general engagements. armour-Piercing (AP): Effective against lightly armoured vehicles and personnel. Tracer: Used to allow the gunner to see the trajectory of the rounds, particularly in low-light conditions. Capability: The .30 caliber M1919A4 machine gun was fed by a belt of ammunition, typically in 250 or 500-round belts. It was effective in suppressing infantry and light vehicle targets, with the tracer rounds helping gunners track their fire and adjust their aim. The armour-piercing rounds were useful for engaging lightly armoured enemy vehicles or emplacements.

    • Rate of Fire (M1919A4 and M2 .50 Caliber)

  • Fire Rates: M1919A4: 400-600 rounds per minute. M2 .50 Caliber: 450-600 rounds per minute. Capability: Both the M1919A4 and M2 had high rates of fire, which made them highly effective for suppressive fire and keeping enemy infantry pinned down. The rapid fire capability of the M1919A4 was particularly useful for infantry support, while the M2 .50 caliber machine gun’s rate of fire allowed it to engage both infantry and lightly armoured vehicles with consistent effectiveness.
