The M4 Sherman's tracks were a key component that contributed to its mobility and effectiveness on the battlefield. The wide design, link-and-block construction, and ability to distribute weight over a larger surface area allowed the Sherman to cross soft terrain and obstacles more easily than many of its contemporaries. The T66 steel tracks improved durability, making the Sherman more effective in off-road combat. While the tracks required maintenance and frequent repairs, they played a major role in ensuring that the Sherman could operate across various terrains in both offensive and defensive operations.

    Track Type

  • Type: T49 rubber-block tracks (standard), later replaced by T66 steel tracks in some variants. Capability: The Sherman initially used T49 rubber-block tracks, which offered a balance between road mobility and durability. The T66 steel tracks, introduced later, were more durable and better suited for off-road conditions, providing improved traction and longevity, especially in harsher environments like mud or snow. These tracks were designed to distribute the tank's weight more evenly, reducing ground pressure and improving mobility on soft terrain.

    • Track Width

  • Type: 16 inches (406 mm). Capability: The wide track design (16 inches) helped distribute the weight of the Sherman tank over a larger surface area, reducing the ground pressure and improving the tank's ability to cross soft ground, such as mud or sand. This feature made the Sherman more mobile in rough terrains, allowing it to navigate obstacles more easily than tanks with narrower tracks.

    • Track Construction

  • Type: Steel with rubber inserts (T49) or full steel (T66). Capability: The T49 tracks used a steel core with rubber inserts to reduce noise and wear on roads, making it more suitable for road travel and reducing vibrations for the crew. Later models with T66 tracks were made entirely of steel, offering greater durability for cross-country movement and better overall wear resistance, but at the cost of more noise and vibration.

    • Track Length

  • Length: Approximately 12 feet (3.66 meters). Capability: The long track length contributed to the Sherman's ability to maintain stability and mobility on difficult terrain. A longer track allows for better weight distribution, helping the tank stay afloat on soft ground (like mud or snow), reducing the likelihood of becoming stuck. The longer track length also improved the overall traction and climbing ability of the tank over obstacles.

    • Track Life and Durability

  • Durability: Undurable with a relatively short lifespan, requiring frequent replacement in combat. Capability: The Sherman's tracks were relatively durable, but they did wear out over time, especially during prolonged combat operations in harsh environments. The track design, particularly with the T66 steel tracks, meant that they required regular replacement due to wear from rough terrain, high speeds, and the stresses of combat. However, the Sherman's tracks were designed to be replaced in the field, ensuring that the tank could maintain mobility even after suffering track damage.
